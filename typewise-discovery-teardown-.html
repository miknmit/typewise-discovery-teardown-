The sharpest problem

The traffic you have is for the wrong product

Six gaps between the product and its discovery

For “best AI customer service platform 2026”, the listicles and the AI summary named Kore.ai, Zendesk, Cognigy, Sierra, eesel, Fin, Gorgias, Ada, and Decagon. Typewise appeared in none of them — absent from the active buyer's very first shortlist.

“Zendesk alternatives” is the biggest migration-intent journey in support software. Typewise publishes its own alternatives post but is cited in no third-party list — and third-party lists are what drive both rankings and AI citations. You can't be the alternative buyers consider if the lists don't name you.

Ask an LLM directly for "Typewise vs Intercom Fin vs Zendesk" and it gives a clean, fair comparison — Typewise is well represented (AI agent + assist, broad CRM integrations like Salesforce/Zendesk/ServiceNow, enterprise-grade privacy). So the models know Typewise. The gap is upstream: in the unbranded discovery queries a buyer actually starts with — "best AI customer service platform," (longtail) "Zendesk alternatives"(shorttail) — Typewise doesn't surface at all. So the only way a buyer reaches your head-to-head is if they already know your name. You win the comparison; you're just never entered into it. And even inside that comparison, the model cedes the category's high ground: it frames Fin as "AI-first… designed for autonomous ticket resolution" while casting Typewise as the AI that "works with your existing stack". That's the safe bolt-on slot — not the resolution leader you actually are. So the fix is two-layered: earn presence in the unbranded answers, and steer how the models frame you once you're there.

The 2026 category splits on deflection vs. resolution and buyer-friendly per-resolution pricing. Typewise does both — autonomous resolution across CRM/ERP/billing, $1-per-resolution pricing. The winning message is sitting unused while louder competitors own the language.

Organic traffic (~1.9K/mo on a domain with 31.5K backlinks) is almost entirely legacy keyboard content — the top page is “17 smartphone keyboard questions,” top keywords are “phone keyboard,” “kika keyboard,” “go keyboard emoji.” ICP pages draw 6–16 visits.

Why it hurts: topical dilution weakens CS rankings; the corpus mistrains LLMs so “keyboard” pollutes the brand's machine-representation; and content effort is spent on non-buyers.

The fix is triage, not deletion: preserve legacy link equity via redirects/consolidation, prune or de-index pure-consumer pages, stop new keyboard content, and rebuild topical clusters around customer service so Google and the LLMs re-classify the domain.

This is very fixable. The foundation exists: real enterprise proof (DPD ~30% efficiency, IVECO ~25% workload automation, 10M+ tickets, 200+ integrations), G2 badges, EU AI Act / GDPR / ISO 27001 positioning, 31.5K backlinks, and a blog with comparison content. The assets are just aimed at the wrong audience and not distributed where citations are made.

What I'd do

A focused first 30 days

  1. Fix the foundation first. Segment ICP vs non-ICP traffic, then triage the legacy keyboard content so the domain starts re-ranking (and being re-read by LLMs) as a customer-service brand.
  2. Own the comparison & alternative queries. Buyer-first comparison pages (vs Fin, Zendesk AI, eesel) engineered to be quoted by LLMs — verdict tables, direct claims, schema, a quotable stat per page.
  3. Close the GEO citation gap. Get Typewise into the third-party lists and datasets models synthesize: listicle outreach, G2/aggregators, targeted Reddit and community answers, analyst inclusion.
  4. Weaponize the customer proof. Turn DPD/IVECO/Galaxus outcomes into structured, quotable stat pages journalists and models lift verbatim.
  5. Publish the POV you already earn. A founder-led “resolution over deflection / pay per outcome” narrative, so Typewise owns the category language.
  6. Measure what matters. Qualified inbound, demo requests, and share-of-voice in AI answers for the top 20 buyer queries — not vanity traffic.

This audit used public SERPs, AI-answer engines, and a point-in-time Semrush snapshot — external signals, not Typewise's internal analytics. I'd confirm and size it with Search Console and full exports before committing.
